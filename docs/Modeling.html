Predictors

We seek to relate landslide locations to measurable attributes of the terrain. Available data for characterizing landslide locations on Wrangell Island are provided by the Tongass National Forest landslide inventory. Available data for characterizing terrain attributes include a lidar-derived DEM and the Tongass National Forest datasets on soils, land cover, and history of forest activities. The types of terrain attributes associated with landsliding can be inferred from physical models of slope instability and from the results of other empirical studies of landslide susceptibility. These studies point to a set of key topographic attributes: slope gradient, local curvatures, and upslope contributing area, all of which can be measured directly or inferred from the DEM. Properties of the soil, including depth, porosity, bulk density, hydraulic transmissivity, friction angle, and cohesion, also play key roles. These properties are not measurable with remotely sensed data, but variations in these properties may be inferred from soil-type mapping.

Topographic Indices

We can calculate a variety of topographic indices using the lidar DEM. As mentioned above, topographic attributes expected to be associated with landslide initiation zones include slope gradient, curvature, and flow accumulation. Gradient and curvature are scale-dependent, in that the value can vary with the length over which they are measured. These properties of the topography influence patterns and rates of movement for water and colluvium, thus influencing the depth of soil and the depth of soil saturation during rainstorm and snowmelt events. The pertinent length scales over which these patterns influence soil stability are reflected in the size distribution of the initiation zones. I calculated gradient and curvatures over a 30-meter length, equal to the mean of the initiation-zone widths (Figure 2).

The upslope flow-accumulation zone provides a relative measure of the amount of water flowing to the area represented by a DEM cell during rainstorms. Once subsurface flow through the soil reaches steady state, the discharge of water through a DEM cell is the upslope accumulation area times the rainfall intensity. This steady-state condition may never actually be achieved, so it may be informative to look at accumulation zones associated with storms of a fixed duration. These are calculated with program Partial, which I have prerun for a range of storm durations from 6 to 72 hours. This is an approximate solution using Darcy flow velocity for shallow subsurface flow, assuming that saturated water flow through the soil is parallel to the ground surface and ignoring the time for water to infiltrate to the saturated zone.

I also examine predictions of a simple physical model as a potential predictor, using the Factor of Safety FoS calculated with the infinite slope model assuming uniform soil properties. This model is an abstraction of actual physical conditions that ignores many factors pertinent to soil failure, so the modeled values may not provide a direct estimate of how landslide potential varies spatially. It does, however, provide a single-valued function that incorporates both slope gradient and flow accumulation. I’ll examine the FoS calculated using flow accumulation rasters generated for a 6-hour and 72-hour storm.

There are several options for exploring the degree to which different indices are associated with landslide initiation zones. A starting point is to look at the frequency distribution of index values both inside the initiation zones and over the entire study area. The degree to which these differ indicates the degree to which an index can differentiate initiation zones from the area in general. These distributions are plotted below using both bar plots and cumulative distributions.

To calculate these distributions, I masked the study area to include only those areas that fell within the range of gradient, FoS, and curvature values found within the initiation zones. Values outside of these zones have a landslide density of zero, based on this inventory. I want to focus on those areas of the landscape where landslide density is greater than zero.

For areas falling within any increment of an index value, the proportion of that area that is within the intiation zones provides a measure of landslide density for that increment. Program SamplePoints will extract these areas for each index. The following code chunks run SamplePoints and generate a series of plots showing histograms and cumulative frequency distributions of variable values both inside the initiation zones and over the entire study area. The landslide density associated with each binned inverval of variable values is also shown, with a smoothed line fit through the points. Density is calculated as the initiation-zone area divided by the total area within each interval range. The same vertical scale is used for all the density plots so that they can be compared directly.

ROC and AUC

Receiver operator curves (ROC) and area under the ROC curve (AUC) provide means to compare the ability of different predictors to differentiate areas with high landslide density from areas with lower landslide density.

For any of the indices, the integration of density over the study are gives the total area of initiation zones:

\[ A_{initiation} = \int_{A_T}\rho(s)ds \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n}a\rho_i \tag{1}\]

where \(A_{initiation}\) is the cumulative area of all initiation zones, \(A_T\) is total study area, and \(\rho(s)\) is spatially variable landslide density. This integral is estimated by summing the value of \(\rho\) for each DEM cell (from the histograms above) times cell area \(a\). Performing the sum over ordered \(\rho\) values shows what proportion of the study area falls at or below (or above) a given \(\rho\) value. Limiting the sum only to mapped initiation zones shows what proportion of the total initiation-zone area falls at or below (or above) a given \(\rho\) value. Plotting the proportion of initiation-zone area as a function of proportion of total study area shows what proportion of study area contains a given proportion of the mapped initiation zones, ranked by landslide density.

These curves are referred to as Receiver-Operator-Characteristic (ROC) curves, or as Success-Rate Curves (Chung and Fabbri 2003). For an index where the frequency distribution of values within the initiation zones is distinctly different than the distribution over the entire study area (masked here to include only index values where landsliding is observed), the ROC curve will start out steeply, indicating that a large portion of the initiation-zone area is contained within a small proportion of the study area. If the two frequency distributions completely overlap, the ROC curve will follow the diagonal line from zero to one, shown by the dashed line in Figure 3.

Correlation

This suggests that we could use gradient or one of the FoS models as relatively good predictors of future landslide location. However, it may be that density varies with certain combinations of indices: that there may be interactions among indices associated with variability in landslide density. For example, we may find that the density associated with FoS_72 varies with gradient, or with PCA_72, or with aspect, or with curvature, or with some combination of other indices. To explore potential interactions, we need to first ensure that the indices themselves are not correlated. For example, the calculation of FoS uses both gradient and contributing area. A change in FoS_72 value reflects changes in gradient and PCA_72 values, so including these other indices may provide little or no information that isn’t already represented by FoS_72. The next code chunk calculates correlations between each pair of index rasters, limited to the masked area.

FoS_72 is, of course, perfectly correlated with itself, but also highly correlated with FoS_6, Gradient, and the 6-hour partial contributing area indices. Correlation with curvature, total accumulation area, and aspect are all low, so interactions of these indices with FoS_72 may be worth exploring.

Directly binning covariate values works adequately for a single predictor. It may be, however, predictors act in combination to determine where landslides occur. Perhaps, for example, the density associated with a calculated FoS value varies with the upslope contributing area. To estimate the density of landslide initiation zones over multiple predictors requires binning in a multidimensional covariate data space. We may find we have insufficient data to adequately fill the bins. I’ll explore two alternatives for calculating landslide density as a function of one or more predictors: the use of spatial point process models, as used for presence-only data analysis, and subsampling of the predictors used to train a classification model.
